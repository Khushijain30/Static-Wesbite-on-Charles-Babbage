Charles Babbage KH FRS was an English polymath who lived from December 26, 1791, to October 18, 1871.Babbage was a mathematician, philosopher, inventor, and mechanical engineer who invented the digital programmable computer.

Some regard Babbage to be the "Father of the Computer." Though Babbage's Analytical Engine, programmed using a principle openly borrowed from the Jacquard loom, is credited with inventing the first mechanical computer, the Difference Engine, that eventually led to more complex electronic designs, all of the essential ideas of modern computers can be found in Babbage's Analytical Engine.

In addition to his work on computers, which he documented in his book Economy of Manufactures and Machinery, Babbage had a wide variety of interests. His diverse work in various subjects has earned him the title of "preeminent" among the century's numerous polymaths.

Babbage, who died before several of his innovations, like his Difference Engine and Analytical Engine, were fully realised, remained a key role in the development of computing. The Science Museum in London has several of Babbage's unfinished machinery on exhibit. Babbage's initial concepts were used to build a working difference engine in 1991. The success of the completed engine, which was built to 19th-century specifications, demonstrated that Babbage's machine would have operated.

Personal Background

Babbage's birthplace is contested, however he was most likely born in 44 Crosby Row, Walworth Road, London, England, according to the Oxford Dictionary of National Biography. The incident is commemorated with a blue plaque at the intersection of Larcom Street and Walworth Road.

His date of birth was published as 26 December 1792 in his obituary in The Times, but a nephew afterwards wrote to indicate that Babbage was born one year earlier, in 1791. Babbage was baptised on January 6, 1792, according to the parish registry of St. Mary's, Newington, London, which supports a birth year of 1791.

Benjamin Babbage and Betsy Plumleigh Teape had four children, including Babbage. In 1801 William Praed founded Praed's & Co. of Fleet Street, London, with his father as a banking partner. The Babbage family relocated to East Teignmouth's ancient Rowdens mansion in 1808. Babbage was transferred to a rural school in Alphington, near Exeter, when he was eight years old to recuperate from a life-threatening sickness. He attended King Edward VI Grammar School in Totnes, South Devon, for a period, but his health prompted him to return to private tutors.

Babbage then went to the Reverend Stephen Freeman's Holmwood Academy, which had 30 students and was located on Baker Street in Enfield, Middlesex. Babbage's passion of mathematics was sparked by the academy's library. After leaving the school, he continued his studies with two additional private instructors.

The first was a pastor near Cambridge, who introduced Babbage to Charles Simeon and his evangelical followers, but the training was insufficient. At the age of 16 or 17, he was sent home to Totnes to study at the Totnes school. The second was an Oxford instructor, who helped Babbage achieve a level of Classics that allowed him to be admitted into Cambridge University.
